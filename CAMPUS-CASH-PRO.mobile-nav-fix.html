💡 Budgeting tip
The 50/30/20 rule. 50% of take-home income to needs (rent, groceries, utilities), 30% to wants (going out, fun, subscriptions), 20% to savings and debt payoff. In college it's okay to adjust the ratios — but be intentional about which bucket gets cut. Most students accidentally let "wants" creep above 50%.

💡 Spending tips
Cook 4 nights a week, save $200/month. At $12 average per meal out vs $4 cooking, switching 4 nights per week saves $160–$210/month. That's $2,000+ per year back in your pocket — from one habit change.
The grocery list is law. Write it before you go. Eat before you shop. Stick to the list. Store brands are made in the same factories as name brands — identical quality, 20–40% cheaper. These three habits alone cut grocery spend by 20–30%.

💡 Savings tips
Emergency fund comes first. Before any other savings goal, build $500–$1,000 in an untouched account. One unexpected expense — a car repair, a medical bill, a last-minute flight home — without this cushion means credit card debt. It's the single most protective thing you can do with your first $500.
Pay yourself first. The day your paycheck hits, move your savings amount out immediately — before you spend anything. What's left is your spending money. Most people do it backwards, spend freely, and wonder why saving feels impossible. Automate it if you can.

💡 Investing tips for students
Start a Roth IRA at 18. If you have any earned income (W-2 job, freelance, 1099, babysitting — anything taxable), you're eligible. Put in even $25–50/month. Money invested at 20 doubles roughly 4 times before retirement. Money invested at 40 doubles twice. Time is the asset — not the amount.
How compound growth actually works. At 8% annual returns, money doubles roughly every 9 years (the Rule of 72). $1,000 at age 20 becomes ~$32,000 by 65. The same $1,000 invested at 40 becomes ~$4,000 by 65. The variable that matters most isn't how much you invest — it's when you start.
HYSA vs. brokerage vs. Roth IRA. Match the account to the time horizon. HYSA (4–5%): money you might need in 1–3 years — liquid, safe, FDIC-insured. Brokerage: money you won't touch for 5+ years — market risk, no contribution limits. Roth IRA: retirement money — post-tax contributions mean growth and withdrawals are completely tax-free.
How to open a Roth IRA as a student. You need earned income (any taxable job counts). You can contribute up to $7,000/year. Fidelity and Schwab have no account minimums and no fees. Opening takes about 20 minutes online. Even $25/month at 20 beats $500/month at 40. The hardest part is just starting — everything else is automatic after that.

💡 Credit and debt tips
Build credit now, thank yourself at 30. Get one student credit card. Use it for one small recurring bill — like Spotify. Set autopay to pay the full balance every month. Never carry a balance. In 4 years you'll have a solid credit history before you need a car loan, apartment, or mortgage.
Graduate with zero credit card debt. This is the one rule that matters most. Pay your full statement balance every single month. Credit card interest runs 20–29% APR — the most expensive debt you can hold. One unpaid month starts a compounding spiral that can follow you for years after graduation.

💡 Subscription audit tip
The subscription audit trick. Open your bank statement and highlight every recurring charge. You'll almost always find 2–4 things you forgot you're paying for. Cancel anything you haven't actively used in the last 30 days — no exceptions, no "I'll use it next month." Set a calendar reminder to do this audit every 3 months. Subscriptions are designed to be invisible; make them visible.

💡 Scholarship strategy tips
Apply to local scholarships. Local awards ($500–$2,000) have far fewer applicants than national ones. A 2-hour application for a $1,000 award is a $500/hr return.
Treat your GPA as a financial asset. Many scholarships require 3.0+. Every grade you protect is potential money you keep.
Set renewal reminders. Losing a $3,000 scholarship because you missed a renewal deadline is one of the most painful and avoidable financial mistakes in college.
Check your department. Most students don't know their own major's department offers small awards. Ask your advisor or department office every semester.

💡 Internship strategy tips
Apply absurdly early. Top firms (FAANG, banks, MBB) open summer apps in Aug–Oct of the prior year and many close before December. By March, the best gigs are gone.
Referrals 4× your odds. A cold app has roughly a 2% callback rate. A referred app is closer to 8–10%. Find one alum on LinkedIn per application — it's the single highest-leverage thing you can do.
Apply broadly, then narrow. The rule of thumb is 30–50 applications to land 1–3 offers. Don't put all your hope on five "dream" companies — spread the net.
Follow up on ghosts. If no response in 2 weeks, send a short polite check-in. About 1 in 4 ghosted apps respond to a follow-up — and any reply is information.
Negotiate even intern offers. Many companies have ranges of $3–7/hr (or $1k–3k in stipend) of flex. The worst they say is "no." Asking nicely never costs you the offer.
Cost-of-living trap. A $35/hr internship in SF and a $22/hr internship in Atlanta net roughly the same after rent. Always run the math before "the bigger number" wins.

How to get a CSV from your bank:
Bank of America: Sign in → click your account → Activity → scroll to find the Download link on the right → choose date range → pick CSV → Download Transactions. (The "Statements & Documents" section gives PDFs — use the Activity page, not Statements.)
Chase (debit or credit): Sign in → choose account → See activity → download icon (↓) at top right → CSV.
Wells Fargo: Sign in → choose account → Download Account Activity → CSV.
Capital One / Discover / Citi (credit cards): Sign in → card account → Statements / Activity → Export → CSV. Switch the toggle above to Credit Card.
American Express: Sign in → Statements & Activity → Download → CSV. Choose Credit Card above.
Other banks: Look for "Download", "Export", or a ↓ icon on your activity / transactions page.
